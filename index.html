// what is monte carlo?

Why one answer isn't really an answer

Imagine you want to guess what the weather will be like on your birthday next year. You could just say "it's usually around 70 degrees, so it'll probably be 70 degrees." Simple — but not very honest. Because sometimes it's 55, sometimes it's 85, and sometimes it rains all day.

A smarter approach: instead of guessing once, you imagine 5,000 different possible birthdays — some hot, some cold, some stormy — and see what happens across all of them. Now you don't just get one answer. You get a picture of the whole range of possibilities.

That's exactly what Monte Carlo simulation does with your money. Instead of assuming the stock market returns exactly 8% every year like clockwork, it "rolls the dice" 5,000 times — each time picking a different sequence of good years, bad years, and mediocre years — and runs your decision through all of them.
📅
The basic calculator
Assumes the market returns exactly 8% every single year. Neat, tidy, and a little dishonest about how the world actually works.
🎲
Monte Carlo
Runs 5,000 imaginary futures. Some years the market is up 25%. Some years it's down 30%. Most years it's somewhere in between — just like real life.
📊
What you get
Not just one answer, but a probability. "Investing wins in 72% of futures." That's a much more honest picture than a single number.

At the end of all 5,000 simulations, this tool counts: how many times did investing win? How many times did paying off the loan win? If investing wins in 4,200 out of 5,000 scenarios, that's an 84% probability — a confident result. If it only wins in 2,600, that's basically a coin flip, and the honest answer is "too close to call."

The "prob. invest wins" number in the metrics above is exactly that count. A high number means investing is the stronger choice across many possible futures. A low number means the guaranteed return of paying off your loan is hard to beat — no matter what the market does.

The finance principles behind this tool

This model is built on three core concepts from corporate finance. Here's what each one means and how it shows up in the numbers above.

⏳
Time Value of Money
A dollar today is worth more than a dollar in the future — because money you have now can be invested and grow. This is why we discount future cash flows back to today's value (present value) and why every dollar of surplus in this model is evaluated based on what it's worth across time.
📋
Capital Budgeting
The process of deciding whether a long-term investment is worth making — do the future benefits outweigh the upfront cost? The key tools are NPV and IRR. In this app, paying off your loan is reframed as a capital budgeting decision: you're evaluating whether putting extra money toward debt beats investing it in the market.
⚖️
Risk-Return
Higher potential returns always come with higher risk. A Treasury bond pays ~5% because it's nearly risk-free. The stock market returns ~8–10% historically because you can lose money in any given year. In this app, loan payoff is the "risk-free" option — a guaranteed return equal to your interest rate — while investing is the risky one.
This is exactly why the Monte Carlo simulation matters. Risk-Return theory tells us the market's average return is just that — an average. The simulation shows you the full range of what could actually happen, so you're not making a decision based on a number that almost never occurs in any single year.
